## Paper A — recommended paragraph

This study applies established practical-identifiability tools to an espresso
extraction case study. Its contribution is not a new identifiability method. Rather,
it separates four questions that are often conflated in model calibration:
localization of the fitted parameters, held-out predictive error, incremental skill
relative to a level-only benchmark, and transfer across grinds and independent
coffee datasets. For the tested multi-solute model, observation map, parameter
domain, and error objectives, whole-cup data leave a broad compensating
inventory–rate profile, whereas fraction-resolved observations localize the rate
more strongly. The result is model- and design-specific and does not establish a
general structural impossibility for cup-integrated observations.

## Paper B — recommended paragraph

This study does not claim a newly discovered espresso mechanism. It evaluates what
matched whole-cup and whole-bed observables can discriminate within a specified set
of published and registry implementations. The contribution is an applied,
null-first comparison: candidate mechanisms are required to improve on simpler
baselines on the same observable, and isolated branches are constrained by the sign
or shape of their predicted contribution where fit quality alone is non-specific.
The conclusions are therefore limited to model capacity, incompatibility, and the
need for temporal flexibility in the tested campaigns. They do not identify a unique
physical mechanism, establish a general stability theorem, or demonstrate transfer
to another machine or coffee.

## Terms to retain

- “applied espresso case study”
- “within the tested model set / campaign / parameterization”
- “practical localization” rather than structural non-identifiability
- “can generate” or “is incompatible with the observed sign” rather than
  “is the mechanism” or “refutes the mechanism”
- “to our knowledge, following a documented scoping search” only after the search
  archive exists

## Terms to remove

- “first,” “novel method,” or categorical absence claims
- “identifies,” “proves,” or “validates” when the evidence is in-sample
- claims that an isolated fixed-pressure resistance branch rules out a coupled
  physical mechanism
- claims that a low endpoint error alone establishes mechanistic transfer
