## Abstract body (1874 characters)

Integrated espresso measurements combine solute extraction, porous-bed flow, and machine response, so distinct mechanisms can produce similar cup-scale signals. We compare candidate models against two published datasets using matched observables, explicit nulls, and declared parameter provenance. For a source-derived extraction-yield response across grinder settings, the selected cell means contain no middle-setting maximum, although the study’s conditional quadratic response surface has an interior vertex. A lognormal static-heterogeneity streamtube ensemble can also generate a small interior maximum for selected closure settings; this establishes model capacity, not channeling identification. For a rising-flow trace at fixed nominal pressure, a machine-only model shows that dip-and-recovery is not diagnostic of bed evolution. A time-varying porosity trajectory improves the 15–95 s reconstruction relative to constant baselines, but a flexible non-mechanistic temporal null performs comparably, indicating a need for time variation rather than a unique mechanism. Across eleven pressures, leave-one-pressure-out refits of the two equilibrium parameters track the shared calibration, while residuals remain strongly structured. An isolated transferred swelling branch has the wrong flow sign under the tested fixed-pressure, resistance-only assumptions; this conditional sign test does not exclude swelling in a coupled bed. Finally, an exploratory uncoupled N-tube model concentrates flow in the near-choke, fixed-total-flow, low-lateral-exchange configuration, but concentration disappears under pressure control or stronger lateral homogenization and is not a stability theorem. The comparisons identify which measurements are non-discriminating and motivate pathway-resolved flow, pressure-step, first-drip, and independently measured bed-state experiments.
