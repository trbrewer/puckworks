# Model card: Mo 2023 swelling extraction

**Paper/thesis:** Mo, Navarini, Suggi Liverani, Ellero, "Modelling swelling effects in real espresso extraction using a 1-dimensional coarse-grained model," J. Food Eng. (2023). DOI 10.1016/j.jfoodeng.2023.111843
**Stage(s):** bed_dynamics, extraction (Carman-Kozeny flow closure internal) · **Kind:** runtime
**Status:** proposed

## Scope and mechanism
Particle-level swelling (diffusive uptake of "additional water" with concomitant isotropic geometric expansion) coupled into the classic Melrose/Li 1-D two-population coarse-grained extraction model. Swelling grows the representative fine and coarse radii, which shrinks bed porosity (bed height fixed), which — at fixed pressure drop via Darcy + Carman-Kozeny — throttles flow. Solute transport inside grains is pure hindered diffusion with a partition-coefficient boundary condition (no surface-dissolution kinetics). Includes an optional plug-flow initial-filling front that delays extraction per depth cell until wetted (fixed-flow-rate case only). Headline result: swelling effects on yield/strength are near-negligible at fixed flow rate but large at fixed Δp, with the fine-grind end most affected.

## Governing equations
Particle swelling (spherical, their Eqs. 3, 40–45): ∂c^w/∂t = D^w (1 − c^w) Δc^w, with c^w the volume fraction of additional water, D^w its in-grain diffusivity; BCs c^w(ℛ, t) = C_M at the (initial-coordinate) surface, zero-gradient at center, c^w(R, 0) = 0. Deformation is isotropic (Eq. 1): dx/dX = [1 − c^w]^(−1/d_n) I; maximum swelling degree (Eq. 8) s_m = [(1 − C_M)^(−1/d_n) − 1] × 100%. Swollen radial coordinate from Eq. 42: r³ = 3∫₀^R ξ² [1 − c^w(ξ)]^(−1) dξ.

In-grain solute diffusion coupled to swelling (their Eqs. 9–17): ∂c/∂t = ∇·(D_p ∇c) − c D^w Δc^w, with D_p = ε_p D_b/(β H_c) and swelling-enhanced grain porosity ε_p = 1 − (1 − ε_p⁰)(1 − c^w) (Eq. 11). Surface BC (Eq. 16): c(ℛ) = c_b/K when c_b/K < c(ℛ⁻), else zero-flux (extraction-only; no re-adsorption). Outflux Eq. 17.

Bed level (their Eqs. 19–24), dispersion dropped: ∂c_b/∂t = −v ∂c_b/∂z + (1/ε_b)(4πr_f² n_f j_f + 4πr_c² n_c j_c) − (∂ε_b/∂t) c_b/ε_b, with v = q/ε_b, time-varying ε_b = 1 − (1 − ε_b⁰)(r_f³/ℛ_f³ ϑ_f + r_c³/ℛ_c³ ϑ_c) (Eq. 21), number densities from Eq. 19. Fixed-Δp mode (Eqs. 25–28): q from Darcy with Carman-Kozeny conductivity D = ε_b³ d²_[3,2]/(72 μ τ² (1 − ε_b)²), d_[3,2] = 2/(ϑ_c/r_c + ϑ_f/r_f), τ = (1/ε_b)^n. Initial-filling front (Eqs. 29–30): dz_f/dt = q/ε_b(z_f); diffusion/swelling switched off below the front (avoids a moving-boundary solve; they bound the resulting artificial-diffusion leak at ≤0.6% of solubles).

Symbols: c_b bulk concentration; q superficial velocity; ϑ_f, ϑ_c fine/coarse volume fractions; ℛ_f, ℛ_c initial representative radii (Eqs. 35–36, harmonic means of the measured PSD split at 100 μm diameter); μ viscosity; L_z bed height. Observables: yield Eq. 37, strength Eq. 38.

Not simplified away by us, but note the authors themselves drop bed dispersion (Eq. 18 → 20), fix bed height (swelling absorbed entirely into ε_b), and neglect erosion, consolidation, and particle migration.

## Calibration and validation offered by the source
(1) Sanity check on permeability: Carman-Kozeny k₀ from Table 1 gives 0.9–6.8×10⁻¹³ m² (Table 2), within the literature ranges they cite (Corrochano 2.6×10⁻¹⁴–3.4×10⁻¹³; Navarini 0.7–4×10⁻¹³). This validates only the flow closure at t = 0, not swelling. (2) Extraction vs experiment: on an illy fixed-flow-rate prototype (patented dispensing valve), 3 powders × 3 flow rates, yield and strength vs beverage mass, K tuned to 0.9; agreement is good only for M_c < 30 g, with the model considerably overestimating both beyond that. The authors say this plainly themselves. (3) The swelling mechanism itself is never validated: C_M is assumed, the experiment is run only in the fixed-flow regime where their own simulations say swelling is nearly invisible, and the large fixed-Δp swelling effects (Figs. 3–4) rest on zero data. Validation of the headline claim is therefore absent, not just weak. Fixed-flow experimental trends captured: slower q → higher yield/strength at same M_c; finer grind → higher yield (monotonic — they do NOT reproduce Cameron's non-monotonic fine-grind dip, plausibly because a fixed-flow machine defeats clogging by raising pressure).

## Assumptions and validity range
- Spherical, non-interacting particles; grain centers fixed; bed height fixed, so swelling only reduces ε_b — authors flag this as a very strong assumption (valid only for closed capsules or high tamp pressure).
- Single lumped solute, single K, single D_p; no surface dissolution kinetics (contrast Cameron); no multi-component chemistry.
- No erosion, migration, or pressure consolidation; isothermal; 1-D axially uniform (no channeling/heterogeneity).
- Swelling magnitude assumed at 3.6% size; literature spans 0–15%, mechanism unresolved.
- Extraction-only surface BC (concentration clamp with rectifier) — no resorption regime.
- Validated regime: fixed flow rate 2–4 mL/s, illy powders (d_[3,2] 76–201 μm), ε_b⁰ = 0.17, M_c < 30 g. Silent/unvalidated: fixed-Δp behavior (their central claim), M_c > 30 g, non-illy grinds, any direct swelling measurement, temperature dependence.

## Interface mapping
Inputs consumed: GrindState (fines_fraction → ϑ_f; boulder_radius_m → ℛ_c; needs a fines-radius field or the 100-μm-split convention as an adapter), BedState (porosity, depth_m, area_m2; produces time-varying porosity and k internally), MachineState (P_of_t for fixed-Δp mode; fixed-q mode needs a flow-profile variant of MachineState — minor contract extension). Outputs produced: ShotResultState (EY_pct, tds_pct/strength, traces), plus ε_b(t), k(t) traces.
Couplings: runtime, two-way — per-layer representative-particle PDEs (fine + coarse, ~30 shells each) feed source terms and porosity into the bed convection solve each step; bed c_b feeds particle BCs. This is exactly the kappa(t) = kappa₀·f(...) runtime coupling the backlog asks for, with f supplied by a swelling mechanism. Adapter needed to reconcile Carman-Kozeny k with our Wadsworth/Cameron-informed BedState.k (constant-offset or replace-closure decision at config time).

## Overlaps and conflicts
- **cameron2020.extraction_bdf (extraction, runtime):** same Melrose/Li 1-D two-population lineage but lower-fidelity solute physics (no dissolution kinetics, lumped K, tuned). Does NOT supersede Cameron; competes on the extraction stage and loses on validation depth. Its added value is orthogonal: the swelling → ε_b(t) → k(t) machinery.
- **Open backlog, bed_dynamics kappa(t):** direct hit. Swelling is one of the two named mechanisms ("compaction/swelling") competing to explain the rising-flow residual — note swelling predicts *falling* flow at fixed Δp (Fig. 3a), so as an explanation of a *rising*-flow residual it enters with the wrong sign and would need to be the suppressed competitor, not the driver. Worth having in the registry precisely to make that discrimination runnable.
- **Open backlog, infiltration↔extraction coupling:** their filling-front switch (Eqs. 29–30, extraction gated on front passage) is a cheap implementation of exactly this backlog item, and their Fig. 5 quantifies the early-strength transient it fixes — but only for fixed q. Foster's front (foster2025.infiltration) is the higher-fidelity replacement (capillarity, pump, headspace); Mo's technique shows how to couple whichever front into extraction.
- **wadsworth2026.permeability (packing, calibration):** competing k closure (Carman-Kozeny + tortuosity power law vs percolation form with angularity). Both untested in the tamped fine-grind regime; a config could swap closures under the same swelling dynamics.
- **brewer2026.streamtube (bed_dynamics):** complementary-competing: streamtube explains flow anomalies via static radial heterogeneity, Mo via temporal porosity evolution; a bed can have both. Mo's 1-D homogeneity is silent on channeling.
- **foster2025 competing-hypothesis note:** unaffected — Mo assumes instantaneous initial wetting causes no swelling and doesn't model unsaturated flow.
